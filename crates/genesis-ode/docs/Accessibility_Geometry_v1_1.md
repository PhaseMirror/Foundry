Abstract
How do observers navigate future-state space?

This paper introduces Accessibility Geometry, an exploratory framework for investigating how
observers reconstruct, evaluate, and navigate accessible futures within constrained information
environments. The framework distinguishes between possibility and accessibility, proposing that
observers do not act upon all possible futures but rather upon those futures that remain visible,
reachable, and meaningful from a given observer coordinate.

Observers are defined functionally rather than materially, allowing the framework to remain
applicable across biological, social, organizational, institutional, computational, and other
information-processing substrates. Accessibility is interpreted as navigation within geometry-
defined boundary conditions rather than unrestricted possibility space. The framework further
proposes that observer behavior is scale-dependent and may be investigated through quantized
observer scales, enabling common dynamics to be examined across multiple levels of
organization while remaining substrate agnostic.

Several candidate variables are introduced, including Accessibility, Reconstructability,
Integration, Perceived Observer Value, Shared Geometry, Trust, Geometric Cohesion, Constraint
Preservation, and Homeostatic Stewardship. Exploratory toybox investigations suggest that
Integration functions as a particularly influential variable, repeatedly appearing in learning,
synchronization, adaptation, and recomputation processes. Additional observations suggest that
higher-order observer structures may emerge through coupling, shared geometry, and phase
alignment among lower-order observers, motivating the broader research program of Multi-
Scale Observer Dynamics (MSOD).

The framework further proposes that higher-order observer geometries frequently function as
nested expansions of lower-order geometries rather than replacements for them. Continuity
therefore requires more than information transfer; it requires the preservation, assimilation, and
recomputation of scale-appropriate geometry across time and observer scales.

The present work does not claim empirical validation or universal applicability. Instead, it
proposes a structured set of concepts, variables, and candidate relationships intended to support
future investigation into observer navigation, emergence, continuity, adaptation, and persistence
across scales.




1. Introduction
1.1 The Navigation Problem
How do observers navigate future-state space?

This question appears across a wide range of domains, including psychology, decision theory,
organizational behavior, artificial intelligence, governance, and systems science. Observers
routinely evaluate possible futures, assign significance to alternative trajectories, and select
actions under conditions of uncertainty. Despite extensive study, there remains no broadly
applicable framework for describing how navigation occurs across multiple observer scales
while remaining independent of any specific substrate.

Most existing approaches focus on optimization, prediction, or decision-making. While useful,
such approaches frequently assume that all possible futures are equally available to the observer.
In practice, observers rarely act upon all possibilities. Instead, they act upon a subset of futures
that remain visible, reachable, meaningful, and admissible from their current position.

This observation motivates the present work.




1.2 From Possibility to Accessibility
The framework proposed here begins with a distinction between possibility and accessibility.

Possibility describes futures that may exist.

Accessibility describes futures that an observer can meaningfully perceive, evaluate, and pursue.

Observers therefore navigate accessibility spaces rather than possibility spaces.

This distinction appears simple but has important implications. Two observers may inhabit the
same environment while possessing radically different accessibility landscapes. Opportunities
visible to one observer may remain invisible to another. Likewise, trajectories that are
theoretically possible may remain inaccessible due to information limits, resource constraints,
observer position, or scale.

The central proposal of Accessibility Geometry is that navigation occurs within accessibility-
defined geometry rather than within unrestricted possibility space.




1.3 Multi-Scale Observer Dynamics
During development of the framework, similar patterns repeatedly emerged across multiple
scales of observation.

Examples included:

   •   individual adaptation,
   •   interpersonal relationships,
   •   organizations,
   •   institutional systems,
   •   federated structures.

Although these domains differ substantially, they frequently exhibited recurring processes
involving:

   •   reconstruction,
   •   integration,
   •   synchronization,
   •   coordination,
   •   continuity preservation,
   •   adaptive recomputation.

This observation motivated the broader concept of:

Multi-Scale Observer Dynamics (MSOD)

MSOD is proposed as a substrate-agnostic research program investigating how observers
interact, couple, synchronize, preserve continuity, and generate higher-order observer structures
across scales.

Accessibility Geometry may be interpreted as one component of this broader effort.

1.6 Functional Definition of Observer
The framework adopts a functional definition of observer.

An observer is any system capable of:

   •   reconstructing state,
   •   updating internal models,
   •   projecting future states,
   •   evaluating trajectories,
   •   selecting actions.

This definition intentionally remains independent of substrate.

The framework therefore does not require observers to be biological, conscious, human, or
intelligent in any specific sense.

The defining characteristic is not composition.

The defining characteristic is participation in future-state navigation.

The minimum viable observer is the simplest system satisfying all five functional criteria: it must
reconstruct some state, update internal models in response to new information, project at least
one future state, evaluate trajectories differentially, and select actions based on that evaluation.
This definition intentionally includes simple biological systems and potentially simple
computational systems while excluding purely reactive systems that do not update internal
models. Whether full conscious experience is necessary for observer status remains an open
question that the framework explicitly does not resolve.




1.7 Evidence Tiers and Toybox Methodology
To maintain clear distinctions between observation, interpretation, and speculation, the
framework employs three evidence tiers.

E1 — Direct Observation

Observations directly produced by toybox investigations or explicitly described phenomena.

E2 — Model Interpretation

Interpretations suggested by recurring patterns observed within the framework.

E3 — Speculative Extension

Hypotheses extending beyond currently observed behavior.

Regarding toybox methodology: throughout this paper, toybox investigation refers to structured
thought experiments and systematic conceptual simulations rather than computational code
execution. These investigations involve defining precise initial conditions, varying specific
parameters, observing which variables appear load-bearing, and documenting recurring patterns.

Results should therefore be interpreted as E1 observations and E2 interpretations rather than
computational validation.

Future work should formalize these investigations as executable simulations with documented
code and reproducible outputs.




1.8 Purpose and Scope
Accessibility Geometry does not attempt to provide a complete theory of consciousness, society,
organizations, governance, or reality itself.

Instead, it proposes a structured language for investigating how observers:

   •   reconstruct information,
   •   integrate experience,
   •   navigate accessibility landscapes,
   •   preserve continuity,
   •   generate higher-order observer structures,
   •   adapt under changing conditions.

The goal is not closure.

The goal is the creation of a substrate-agnostic framework capable of supporting future
investigation into observer navigation across scales.




1.9 Relationship to Existing Literature
The Accessibility Geometry framework develops independently but intersects with several
established research traditions.

Affordance Theory

Gibson's concept of affordances—action possibilities offered by the environment relative to the
observer's capabilities—is the closest existing concept to Accessibility as developed here.

The key extension is the explicit incorporation of observer scale as a coordinate and the
distinction between accessibility and possibility as navigational environments.

Bounded Rationality and Satisficing

The framework's Sufficiency concept is consistent with Simon's satisficing model: observers
seek geometries that are sufficient rather than optimal.

The framework extends this by treating sufficiency as geometry-dependent and environment-
contingent rather than as a fixed observer property.

Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning

Multi-Scale Observer Dynamics shares structural similarities with hierarchical reinforcement
learning, in which agents operate simultaneously across multiple scales.

The key distinction is that the present framework treats scale as an emergent observer property
rather than a predefined architectural constraint.

Cybernetics and Requisite Variety

Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety is structurally related to the framework's scope-resolution
trade-off and to Constraint Preservation.
The Scale Reciprocity Principle proposed later in this manuscript may be interpreted as a
distributed expression of requisite variety across observer scales.

Organizational Cognition and Sensemaking

The concept of Shared Geometry parallels Weick's sensemaking framework, in which shared
interpretive structures support coordinated action.

The distinction between Shared Geometry and Geometric Cohesion extends this by separating
the existence of shared structure from its operational integrity.

Panarchy and Adaptive Cycles

The framework's treatment of reindexing shares structural similarities with adaptive-cycle
models in which systems move through exploitation, conservation, release, and reorganization
phases.

The present framework proposes a more granular account of reorganization through the
sequence:

Reconstruction → Verification → Falsification → Integration → Recomputation.

These connections do not constitute prior art for the specific claims of Accessibility Geometry.

They are provided to help situate the framework within the broader landscape of systems,
cognition, and adaptive-governance research.

Observers do not interact directly with reality.

3.11 Zeno Conditions: The Framework's Primary Falsifiable
Prediction
Observers possessing high reconstructability and low integration frequently exhibited recurring
informational loops in toybox investigations.

The framework refers to these conditions as:

Zeno Conditions
Observers trapped in Zeno Conditions often appear capable of identifying problems while
remaining unable to generate viable replacement trajectories.

The resulting pattern resembles repeated falsification without successful recomputation.

Zeno Conditions represent the framework's most precisely testable prediction.

Falsifiable Prediction

Observers exhibiting high reconstructability scores and low integration scores should show:

   •   higher rates of behavioral stagnation,
   •   repeated pattern recognition without behavioral change,
   •   lower rates of successful trajectory recomputation,

than observers possessing comparable reconstructability but higher integration.

This prediction is testable across:

   •   clinical settings,
   •   organizational settings,
   •   computational settings.

Candidate Measurement Approaches

In clinical settings, Zeno Conditions may correspond to the familiar pattern of:

Insight without change.

Patients may accurately describe dysfunctional patterns while remaining unable to modify
behavior.

In organizational settings, post-mortem analyses that repeatedly identify problems without
producing policy change may represent institutional Zeno Conditions.

In computational settings, systems exhibiting high predictive accuracy but low adaptive update
rates may exhibit analogous dynamics.

The framework does not predict that all stagnation conditions are identical.

Rather, it predicts that the specific configuration:

[
High;R_o + Low;I_g
]
produces a distinguishable signature.

This distinguishability constitutes the core testable claim.

Accessibility alone does not explain navigation.

An observer may possess multiple accessible trajectories while pursuing only a small subset of
them.

The existence of an accessible future does not guarantee that the observer will invest resources
toward it.

This observation motivates a second question:

Why is one trajectory selected while another is ignored?

The framework proposes that navigation depends not only upon accessibility but also upon
observer-assigned value.

17.19 Multi-Scale Observer Dynamics
Accessibility Geometry began as an exploration of future-state navigation.

The resulting framework increasingly suggests that observer systems must continuously:

   •   reconstruct,
   •   integrate,
   •   preserve,
   •   transfer,
   •   and recompute geometry

across both scale and time.

These questions substantially exceed the scope of the present work and motivate the broader
Multi-Scale Observer Dynamics research program.

Future work should focus on disciplined investigation rather than premature expansion.

The purpose of the framework is not to provide final answers.
The purpose is to establish a structured language through which increasingly complex questions
may be explored.




17.20 Closing Observation
The framework began with a deceptively simple question:

How do observers navigate future-state space?

The resulting investigation repeatedly returned to broader questions involving:

   •   adaptation,
   •   continuity,
   •   stewardship,
   •   learnability,
   •   persistence.

Whether Accessibility Geometry ultimately proves useful will depend not upon the elegance of
its concepts, but upon whether those concepts help observers better understand how adaptive
systems remain capable of discovering viable trajectories as reality changes.

That question remains open.

It is also the reason the research program continues.
