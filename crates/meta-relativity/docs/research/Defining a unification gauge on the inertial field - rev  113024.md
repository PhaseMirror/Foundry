                                   The Current State of Art

The current state of scientiﬁc investigation and understanding of physical phenomena at a
fundamental level principally consists of general relativity (GR) as the study of macrocosmic
interactions responding to the laws of gravity operating in an observational ﬁeld of spacetime
and of quantum theory (QT) as the study of microcosmic interactions of individual particles
exhibiting inherent properties of rest mass, electromagnetic charge, and spin angular
momentum, referred to and modeled as quanta. These quantum properties are inferred as
such by the results of experimental methods, based on the correlated macrocosmic
properties recognized in aggregate forms comprised of molecular and atomic, elemental
materials. These correlations between GR and QT, both of which are generally formulated as
ﬁeld theories, are generally, and in many cases speciﬁcally, well understood. An exception
in correlated understanding is the gravitational interaction, as the primary domain of GR.

As currently understood, the fundamental nature of the gravitational ﬁeld of GR is the
spacetime continuum, modeled as a four-dimensional manifold, in which there are three
dimensions of space and one dimension of time. By topological deﬁnition, a manifold is a
ﬁeld—an extension of a property in one or more dimensions—that is locally diPerentiable at
all points within it. This makes a manifold continuous at any scale, and technically speaking,
without any boundary understood to be asymptotic, since an asymptote is not deﬁnable by
the properties of the ﬁeld at that asymptote.

probabilistic presence—of an ordered assortment of the inferred quantum properties scaled

fermionic particle is generally locatable in spacetime and has the properties of rest mass,
charge, and intrinsic spin, while bosonic particles travel through spacetime as a means of
interaction conveying momentum and energy between fermions and have the property of
invariant speed of transmission.
