Multiplicity Theory leverages prime-based encoding and multiset theory to model complex systems where stability, identity preservation, and recursive interactions are paramount. \subsection{Prime-Based Encoding and Stability in Recursive Systems}

Prime-based encoding assigns unique prime numbers to each element of a system, such that every component is both identifiable and stable under recursive feedback. This assignment mimics unique identification codes in communication systems, where distinct codes prevent interference. By assigning each component a prime, Multiplicity Theory stabilizes interactions, as no prime factor overlaps with another, ensuring distinctiveness across recursive feedback loops.

Consider a system where each element \( a_i \) is mapped to a unique prime \( p_i \). The encoding function \( \phi \) maps elements to primes:

\begin{equation}
\phi(a_i) = p_i.
\end{equation}

For a multiset \( M = \{(a_1, m_1), (a_2, m_2), \ldots, (a_n, m_n)\} \), where \( m_i \) is the frequency (or multiplicity) of \( a_i \), the prime-based encoding is represented as:

\begin{equation}
M = \{p_1^{m_1}, p_2^{m_2}, \ldots, p_n^{m_n}\}.
\end{equation}

This encoding ensures that each element's identity and frequency are preserved across interactions, establishing a stable foundation for recursive operations. The use of primes as identifiers and stabilizers inherently prevents factorization disruptions, a common issue in recursive systems with traditional additive encoding \citep{gauss1801, freeman2004}.

\subsection{Multiset Interaction Theorem in Recursive Feedback Systems}

Multiplicity Theory models recursive feedback through the multiplicative aggregation of prime-labeled elements, preserving both identity and frequency in recursive dynamics. For two interacting components represented by \( p_i^{m_i} \) and \( p_j^{m_j} \) in a recursive loop, their interaction is captured by:

\begin{equation}
p_i^{m_i} \cdot p_j^{m_j} = p_i^{m_i + m_j}.
\end{equation}

This operation retains the unique identities of each prime-labeled component, ensuring stability as recursive interactions amplify. As feedback loops intensify, the exponents of the prime labels increase proportionally, preserving both stability and component identity. Consider two multisets \( M_1 = \{p_1^{m_1}, p_2^{m_2}, \ldots, p_n^{m_n}\} \) and \( M_2 = \{p_1^{n_1}, p_2^{n_2}, \ldots, p_n^{n_n}\} \). Their cumulative effect in a recursive feedback loop is given by:

\begin{equation}
M_1 \times M_2 = \{p_1^{m_1 + n_1}, p_2^{m_2 + n_2}, \ldots, p_n^{m_n + n_n}\}.
\end{equation}

This cumulative multiplication maintains system stability and identity, as shared elements aggregate while maintaining their unique prime identities, representing interconnected dynamics across dimensions \citep{blizard1989, kumar1990}.

\subsection{Prime-Based Eigenvalues for Stability in Interaction Matrices}

Within Multiplicity Theory, prime numbers serve as both eigenvalues and stabilizers in interaction matrices for complex systems. The stability of recursive systems is ensured by assigning prime numbers as eigenvalues in the interaction matrix, where each eigenvalue remains indivisible and thus resistant to factorization-based disruptions. This method provides a distinct advantage over traditional real or complex eigenvalues, which lack the indivisible property of primes.

\textbf{Theorem: Stability of Prime-Based Eigenvalues in Recursive Systems}

Given an interaction matrix \( M \) with prime-based entries \( M_{ij} = p_i \cdot p_j \), stability is ensured if each eigenvalue \( \lambda_i = p_i \) is a prime number. This condition prevents destabilization through factorization, as recursive feedback loops do not introduce decomposable elements.

\begin{proof}
1. \textit{Prime-Based Eigenvalue Assignment}: Each prime \( p_i \) uniquely identifies a system component \( i \), allowing the interaction matrix \( M \) to be decomposed as:
   \[
   M = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \mathbf{v}_i \mathbf{v}_i^{\dagger},
   \]
   where \( \mathbf{v}_i \) represents the eigenvector associated with \( p_i \).

2. \textit{Irreducibility and Stability}: Since each \( p_i \) is a prime, it is indivisible by any integer other than 1 and itself. This irreducibility guarantees that each eigenvalue \( \lambda_i = p_i \) cannot be decomposed further, maintaining stability in recursive dynamics \citep{nielsen2010, freeman2004}.

3. \textit{Recursive Feedback and Prime Preservation}: Recursive interactions governed by these prime eigenvalues preserve system stability, as no recursive feedback introduces new factors that could destabilize the original prime-based eigenvalue configuration.
\end{proof}

This theorem illustrates how prime-based eigenvalues act as stabilizers, ensuring stability across recursive interactions. The irreducibility of primes reinforces stability, particularly advantageous in complex quantum and computational models where recursive feedback is critical \citep{gottesman1997, shor1997}.

\subsection{Construction of Prime-Based Interaction Matrices}

In Multiplicity Theory, each interaction within a system is represented by a prime-based interaction matrix \( M \). The following algorithm demonstrates how to construct a prime-based interaction matrix for a network of nodes, assigning each node a unique prime label:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption=Constructing Prime-Based Interaction Matrix]
import numpy as np
from sympy import primerange

# Number of nodes
n_nodes = 10

# Generate the first n primes
primes = list(primerange(1, 100))[:n_nodes]

# Initialize the interaction matrix
interaction_matrix = np.zeros((n_nodes, n_nodes))

# Populate the matrix with prime-based interaction strengths
for i in range(n_nodes):
    for j in range(n_nodes):
        if i != j:
            interaction_matrix[i, j] = primes[i] * primes[j]

print("Prime-Based Interaction Matrix:")
print(interaction_matrix)
\end{lstlisting}

In prime-encoded lattices, the encoding assigns prime multiplicities to the lattice points, thereby increasing the complexity of reversing or decoding these structures.
