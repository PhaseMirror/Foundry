\section*{A. Problem Statement \& Scope}

Current computer vision frameworks struggle with generalization, robustness, and ethical interpretability in noisy or evolving environments. Multiplicity Vision addresses this gap with a recursive, prime-encoded, quantum-enhanced architecture designed for high-dimensional perceptual complexity.

As the volume and dimensionality of visual data continue to increase—driven by applications in satellite imaging, medical diagnostics, autonomous systems, and climate surveillance—conventional convolutional and transformer-based models are exhibiting structural limitations. These architectures assume fixed spatial dependencies, operate on rigid input encodings, and degrade sharply under conditions of occlusion, corruption, or nonstationary statistical drift. In practical terms, this leads to clinical misdiagnoses due to imaging noise, navigation errors in dynamic environments, and brittle performance in high-stakes decision-making scenarios.

Furthermore, most existing systems offer little transparency in how visual decisions are derived. As visual AI systems scale into critical infrastructures (e.g., urban surveillance, wildfire detection, disaster relief triage), the need for interpretable, lawful, and adaptive cognition becomes paramount. Classical pipelines often rely on over-parameterized heuristics, making them vulnerable to both epistemic uncertainty and adversarial exploitation.

Multiplicity Vision reframes visual cognition as a recursive, topologically stabilized tensor process. Instead of static convolutional kernels or positional embeddings, it uses Prime-Indexed Recursive Tensor Mathematics (PIRTM) and the Λₘ constant to encode perceptual dynamics. This allows the architecture to evolve lawfully across multiple scales while maintaining ethical invariants and computational reversibility.

The scope of this work is global. Multiplicity Vision is designed to operate across domains such as real-time medical imaging, climate-informed satellite analysis, autonomous mobility, and ethically governed AI in cities. By enabling lawful recursion, dynamic adaptation, and quantum-compressed inference, it sets the stage for next-generation visual systems that are simultaneously intelligent, interpretable, and just.
